1. Introduction to Direct Current

Direct Current (DC) is the foundation of aircraft electrical systems. Unlike Alternating Current (AC), DC flows in one direction only - from the negative terminal of a power source, through the circuit, and back to the positive terminal. Understanding DC is essential for every avionics technician.

Aircraft DC Systems: Most general aviation and turbine aircraft use either 14V DC (single 12V battery systems) or 28V DC (dual 12V batteries in series or 24V battery systems). These voltages power navigation lights, avionics, fuel pumps, and essential flight instruments.

The Three Fundamental Quantities

Quantity Symbol Unit Description
Voltage V or E Volts (V) Electrical pressure that pushes electrons through a circuit
Current I Amperes (A) Rate of electron flow through a conductor
Resistance R Ohms (Ω) Opposition to current flow

Water Analogy: Think of voltage as water pressure, current as the flow rate (gallons per minute), and resistance as a restriction in the pipe. Higher pressure pushes more water through; a larger restriction reduces flow.

2. Ohm's Law - The Foundation

Ohm's Law is the most fundamental relationship in electrical theory and your most valuable troubleshooting tool. It mathematically relates voltage, current, and resistance.

V = I × R
Voltage (Volts) = Current (Amps) × Resistance (Ohms)

Derived Formulas

Rearranging Ohm's Law gives us three forms:

To Find Formula Use When
Voltage (V) V = I × R You know current and resistance
Current (I) I = V / R You know voltage and resistance
Resistance (R) R = V / I You know voltage and current

Practical Example - Aircraft Landing Light

Problem: A 28V aircraft landing light draws 10 amps. What is the bulb's resistance?

Solution: R = V / I = 28V / 10A = 2.8 Ohms

Application: Knowing this resistance helps you verify bulb condition with an ohmmeter when troubleshooting a light that won't illuminate.

Knowledge Check

A 28V circuit has a total resistance of 7 ohms. How much current flows?

A) 196 Amps
B) 4 Amps
C) 0.25 Amps
D) 35 Amps
Correct! Using I = V/R: 28V / 7Ω = 4 Amps
Incorrect. Use I = V/R: 28V / 7Ω = 4 Amps

3. Series Circuits

In a series circuit, components are connected end-to-end in a single path. Current has only one route to follow, so the same current flows through every component.

Series Circuit Rules

  • Current: Same through all components (IT = I1 = I2 = I3)
  • Voltage: Divides across components (VT = V1 + V2 + V3)
  • Resistance: Adds up (RT = R1 + R2 + R3)
RT = R1 + R2 + R3 + ...
Total Series Resistance = Sum of All Individual Resistances
Kirchhoff's Voltage Law (KVL): The sum of all voltage drops around any closed loop equals the source voltage. This is fundamental to troubleshooting - if you're missing voltage somewhere, you have an open circuit.

Practical Example - Voltage Divider

Problem: A 28V circuit has three resistors in series: 4Ω, 6Ω, and 4Ω. Calculate the voltage drop across the 6Ω resistor.

Solution:

  1. Total Resistance: RT = 4 + 6 + 4 = 14Ω
  2. Current: I = V/R = 28V / 14Ω = 2A
  3. Voltage across 6Ω: V = I × R = 2A × 6Ω = 12V

Critical Fault: Open Circuit in Series

  • If ANY component opens (breaks) in a series circuit, ALL current stops
  • This is why older Christmas lights went completely dark when one bulb burned out
  • In aircraft, a broken wire in a series circuit disables the entire circuit
  • Troubleshooting: Check for continuity through each component and wire segment

Knowledge Check

Three resistors (10Ω, 20Ω, 30Ω) are connected in series. What is the total resistance?

A) 20 Ohms
B) 5.45 Ohms
C) 60 Ohms
D) 6000 Ohms
Correct! RT = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60Ω
Incorrect. In series, resistances add: RT = 10 + 20 + 30 = 60Ω

4. Parallel Circuits

In a parallel circuit, components are connected across common points, creating multiple paths for current. This is how most aircraft electrical systems are wired - so one failed component doesn't disable everything else.

Parallel Circuit Rules

  • Voltage: Same across all branches (VT = V1 = V2 = V3)
  • Current: Divides among branches (IT = I1 + I2 + I3)
  • Resistance: Total is LESS than smallest branch
1/RT = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
Reciprocal Formula for Parallel Resistance

Shortcut for Two Resistors:

RT = (R1 × R2) / (R1 + R2)
Product Over Sum - Works for exactly two resistors
Kirchhoff's Current Law (KCL): The total current entering a junction equals the total current leaving it. Current doesn't "disappear" - it splits among parallel branches proportionally to their conductance.

Practical Example - Aircraft Bus Loading

Problem: Three avionics units are connected in parallel to a 28V bus. Unit A draws 2A, Unit B draws 3A, Unit C draws 5A. What is the total current from the bus?

Solution: IT = 2A + 3A + 5A = 10A

Application: This determines circuit breaker size. A 10A circuit breaker would barely handle this load; a 15A breaker provides margin for inrush current.

Troubleshooting Scenario: Parallel Circuit Short
Symptom: Circuit breaker trips immediately when master switch is turned on. Three navigation lights are on this circuit.
Analysis: A short in ANY parallel branch draws excessive current, tripping the breaker. Since voltage is the same across all branches, a short (near-zero resistance) causes extremely high current in that branch.

Procedure: Disconnect each light one at a time and reset breaker. When breaker holds, you've found the shorted branch. Check that bulb, socket, and wiring for short to ground.

Knowledge Check

Two 10Ω resistors are connected in parallel. What is the total resistance?

A) 5 Ohms
B) 20 Ohms
C) 10 Ohms
D) 100 Ohms
Correct! RT = (10 × 10)/(10 + 10) = 100/20 = 5Ω
Incorrect. Use product over sum: (10 × 10)/(10 + 10) = 5Ω

5. Electrical Power (Watt's Law)

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is converted to another form (heat, light, motion). Understanding power is critical for proper wire sizing, circuit protection, and preventing thermal damage.

P = V × I
Power (Watts) = Voltage (Volts) × Current (Amps)

Derived Power Formulas

By substituting Ohm's Law, we get additional useful formulas:

Formula Use When You Know Example Application
P = V × I Voltage and Current Reading bus voltage and ammeter
P = I² × R Current and Resistance Wire heating calculations
P = V² / R Voltage and Resistance Load power with known resistance

Practical Example - Circuit Breaker Selection

Problem: A landing light is rated at 250 watts on a 28V system. What size circuit breaker is needed?

Solution:

  1. I = P / V = 250W / 28V = 8.93A
  2. Add 25% safety margin: 8.93 × 1.25 = 11.16A
  3. Select next standard size: 15A circuit breaker

Power Dissipation and Heat

  • Resistors convert electrical energy to heat (P = I²R)
  • Undersized wiring causes excessive heating and potential fire
  • High-resistance connections (corrosion) cause localized heating
  • Always use properly rated components - resistor wattage ratings exist for safety

Knowledge Check

A 28V circuit draws 5 amps. How much power is consumed?

A) 5.6 Watts
B) 140 Watts
C) 33 Watts
D) 0.18 Watts
Correct! P = V × I = 28V × 5A = 140W
Incorrect. P = V × I = 28V × 5A = 140W

6. Multimeter Operation (Fluke 87V)

The digital multimeter (DMM) is your primary diagnostic tool. The Fluke 87V is an industry-standard meter used throughout aviation maintenance. Proper use prevents damage to equipment and ensures accurate measurements.

Measuring Voltage (VDC)

  • Set meter to VDC (DC Volts)
  • Connect probes IN PARALLEL (across the component)
  • Red probe to more positive point, black to more negative
  • Circuit power must be ON
Voltage Measurement Tip: When troubleshooting, measure voltage at multiple points to locate where voltage is lost. Good voltage at the breaker but zero at the component indicates an open between those points.

Measuring Current (Amps)

  • Set meter to A (Amps) - use 10A jack for high current
  • Connect meter IN SERIES (break circuit and insert meter)
  • All current must flow through the meter
  • Use clamp meter for non-invasive measurement when possible

Current Measurement Safety

  • NEVER connect ammeter in parallel - you will create a short circuit
  • Ensure meter is rated for expected current (check fuse rating)
  • Start with highest range if current is unknown
  • Blown meter fuse is the #1 cause of "dead" multimeters

Measuring Resistance (Ohms)

  • Set meter to Ω (Ohms)
  • Disconnect power to circuit - POWER MUST BE OFF
  • Isolate component from circuit to avoid parallel paths
  • Connect probes across component
Reading Indication Common Causes
0 Ω or very low Short circuit / Good conductor Wire continuity OK, or unwanted short
Expected value Component within spec Resistor, bulb, motor windings normal
OL (Over Limit) Open circuit / Infinite resistance Broken wire, blown fuse, burned component

Fluke 87V Specific Features

  • True RMS: Accurate readings on non-sinusoidal waveforms
  • Min/Max/Avg: Capture transient readings
  • Relative Mode: Zero out lead resistance for precision measurements
  • Low-Z Mode: Prevents ghost voltages on disconnected circuits
  • CAT III 1000V / CAT IV 600V: Safety rated for aircraft systems

7. Common DC Faults and Troubleshooting

Understanding fault types and their electrical signatures is essential for efficient troubleshooting. Each fault type produces characteristic symptoms that guide your diagnosis.

Open Circuit

A break in the current path that stops all current flow.

Characteristic Measurement
Resistance across open OL (Infinite)
Current through circuit 0 Amps
Voltage across open Full source voltage
Scenario: Navigation Light Won't Illuminate
Symptom: Right nav light is dark. Left nav and tail work fine. Circuit breaker not tripped.
Troubleshooting Steps:
  1. Check bulb - most common failure (remove and test with ohmmeter)
  2. Measure voltage at light socket - should be 28V
  3. If 0V at socket, trace wire back checking voltage until you find where it's lost
  4. Check for corroded connectors or broken wire in wing root area

Short Circuit

An unintended low-resistance path that allows excessive current flow.

Characteristic Measurement
Resistance of short Near 0Ω
Current through short Very high (limited by source)
Circuit breaker Trips immediately or repeatedly

Short Circuit Dangers

  • Can cause immediate fire if circuit protection fails
  • Damages wiring insulation through overheating
  • May damage connected equipment
  • Never bypass circuit protection to "test" a tripping breaker

High-Resistance Connection

The most insidious fault - often intermittent and hard to find.

Characteristic Symptom
Voltage drop across connection Higher than normal (should be near 0V)
Equipment performance Dim lights, slow motors, intermittent operation
Connection temperature Warm or hot to touch
Visual inspection Corrosion, discoloration, melted plastic

Voltage Drop Testing

The best way to find high-resistance connections:

  1. Circuit must be powered and under load
  2. Measure voltage drop across each connection point
  3. A good connection should show less than 0.1V drop
  4. Higher drops indicate resistance - clean or replace connection

Ground Circuit: Measure voltage from component ground terminal to aircraft structure. Should be near 0V. More than 0.5V indicates poor ground.

Scenario: Dim Landing Light
Symptom: Landing light is noticeably dimmer than normal. Ammeter shows lower than expected current draw.
Analysis: High resistance somewhere in circuit is dropping voltage before it reaches the bulb. Lower voltage = less current = dimmer light.

Troubleshooting:
  1. Measure voltage at the bulb socket - should be 28V
  2. If low (e.g., 24V), the "missing" 4V is being dropped somewhere
  3. Perform voltage drop test on each connection back to breaker
  4. Common culprits: corroded wing root connector, dirty switch contacts

8. Safety Considerations

Electrostatic Discharge (ESD)

  • Modern avionics contain ESD-sensitive components
  • Static discharge as low as 30V can damage CMOS circuits
  • Always use ESD protection: Wrist straps, grounded mats, anti-static bags
  • Ground yourself before touching circuit cards or connectors

Battery Safety

  • Aircraft batteries can deliver hundreds of amps in a short circuit
  • Always disconnect battery before working on main bus wiring
  • Remove rings, watches, and metal jewelry
  • Never lay tools on top of batteries
  • Lead-acid batteries produce hydrogen gas - ensure ventilation
Aircraft-Specific Cautions:
  • Always use approved aircraft wire (MIL-W-22759 or equivalent)
  • Route wiring away from flight controls, fuel lines, and hot areas
  • Use proper wire gauges per AC 43.13-1B guidelines
  • Secure all wiring with appropriate clamps and grommets
  • Document all electrical work in aircraft maintenance records
